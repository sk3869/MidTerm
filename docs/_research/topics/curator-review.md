# Curator Review: Smithsonian Evaluation

## Role
This review evaluates *Common Tech Through The Years* as if conducted by a senior curator at the Smithsonian Institution's National Museum of American History, assessing the site as a proposed digital exhibition.

---

## Executive Summary

The exhibition presents a coherent and educationally sound narrative about personal technology between the 1970s and the 2010s. The curatorial logic is clear: four transformative technologies, each traced from origin through cultural peak. The Bauhaus visual language signals institutional seriousness. The Sage voice builds trust through precision rather than enthusiasm. The Authority principle is applied correctly — sources are named, claims are attributed, and the framing is scholarly.

**Overall assessment: Promising foundation. The following areas require attention before the exhibition opens.**

---

## Strengths

### 1. Narrative Coherence
The four-exhibit structure creates a natural visitor journey. Each exhibit covers a single technology category within a defined era, preventing scope creep and maintaining focus. The timeline arc (1970s–2010s) gives the collection historical grounding.

### 2. Curatorial Voice
The first-person plural ("we selected," "this collection documents") establishes institutional authority without arrogance. The tone is appropriate for a museum context — informative without being academic to the point of exclusion.

### 3. Artifact Selection
The selected artifacts represent genuine historical inflection points, not merely popular devices. The Altair 8800 (not the Apple I) as the PC starting point shows curatorial judgment. The Diamond Rio PMP300 (not the iPod) as the MP3 player origin demonstrates awareness of pre-Apple history.

### 4. Source Attribution
Citing the Computer History Museum, Smithsonian Institution, and Britannica as primary sources establishes the exhibition's scholarly foundation. This is the correct approach for an Authority-driven persuasion strategy.

---

## Areas Requiring Improvement

### 1. Narrative Flow Between Exhibits
**Problem:** The four exhibits currently exist as parallel tracks with no connective tissue. A visitor moving from Personal Computers to Mobile Phones has no guided transition. There is no "reading path" that shows how these technologies influenced each other.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief cross-reference in each exhibit's Cultural Impact section showing how it connects to the others. Example: the PC exhibit should note that miniaturization of PC chips enabled mobile phones.

### 2. Artifact Presentation Depth
**Problem:** Three artifacts per exhibit is minimal for a museum-grade exhibition. The curatorial notes are present but could be more revealing — they should show what is *not* obvious about the artifact.

**Recommendation:** Ensure each curatorial note answers: "What would a casual visitor miss if they only read the significance line?" The note should reveal the non-obvious.

### 3. Cultural Impact Sections
**Problem:** The cultural impact copy is currently planned but not written with enough specificity. Vague impact statements ("this changed how people communicate") are common in amateur exhibits.

**Recommendation:** Each Cultural Impact section should make one specific, verifiable claim. Example: "By 1995, 40% of U.S. households owned a personal computer, up from 8% in 1984 (U.S. Census Bureau)."

### 4. Visitor Orientation
**Problem:** A first-time visitor has no clear sense of where to start or how to navigate.

**Recommendation:** The homepage curatorial statement should include an explicit "suggested path" for first-time visitors. The SiteHeader navigation should make the four exhibit routes immediately visible.

### 5. Visual Evidence
**Problem:** The current design uses text-only artifact presentations. A museum without visual artifacts is a library.

**Recommendation:** In the next iteration, each artifact should have a representative image (sourced from public domain or Creative Commons archives such as the Smithsonian Open Access collection). This is the single highest-impact improvement for visitor engagement.

---

## Emotional Engagement Assessment

The current exhibition scores high on *intellectual engagement* (clear information, credible sources) and low on *emotional engagement* (no sensory connection to the artifacts, no personal resonance hooks).

**Recommendation for improvement:** Add one "Memory Hook" per exhibit — a brief prompt that connects the artifact to personal experience. Example: "If you used a dial-up modem in the 1990s, you already know this sound." This technique is used widely in Smithsonian physical exhibits to bridge historical distance.

---

## Final Recommendation

Approve for midterm review with the understanding that the five improvement areas above form the roadmap for the final review. The strongest version of this exhibition will add cross-exhibit narrative connections, richer cultural impact copy, and — most importantly — visual artifacts sourced from public domain archives.

The process artifacts (specs, QA passes, research memos) demonstrate a disciplined approach to construction. The exhibition concept is sound. The gap between current state and a fully realized digital museum is well-defined and closeable.
