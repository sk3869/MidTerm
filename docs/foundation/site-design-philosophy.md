# Site Design Philosophy

## Overview

This site uses three interlocking design frameworks. Every visual, copy, and interaction decision traces back to one or more of them.

1. **Bauhaus** — visual style
2. **The Sage** — brand archetype
3. **Authority** — Cialdini persuasion principle

---

## 1. Bauhaus Visual Style

### Origin
The Bauhaus school (1919–1933) believed that form follows function. Decoration for its own sake was rejected. Every visual element earns its place by serving a purpose.

### Applied to this site

**Typography**
- One sans-serif typeface family throughout (Inter)
- Hierarchy established through weight and size, not decoration
- Tight letter-spacing on headings; loose line-height on body text
- No italic except for artifact captions and citations

**Color Palette**
- Base: white `#FFFFFF` and near-black `#1A1A1A`
- Accent: Bauhaus red `#D62828`
- Secondary accent: Bauhaus yellow `#F7B731` (used sparingly for callouts)
- No gradients. No shadows except structural separators.

**Layout**
- Strict 12-column grid
- Generous whitespace — content breathes
- Geometric shapes as dividers (horizontal rules, square section markers)
- No rounded corners except interactive elements (buttons, cards on hover)
- Images are always rectangular, never cropped to circles

**Iconography**
- Geometric only. No illustrative icons.
- When in doubt, use text instead of an icon.

---

## 2. The Sage Brand Archetype

### What the Sage is
The Sage seeks truth and shares it. The Sage's role is to educate, illuminate, and guide. The Sage does not entertain — the Sage informs. The Sage's authority comes from knowledge, not power.

### Voice characteristics
- Calm, precise, unhurried
- Uses complete sentences; avoids fragments and exclamation marks
- Favors "this collection documents" over "check out these cool artifacts"
- Cites sources; attributes claims
- Acknowledges complexity without hiding it

### Applied to this site
- Curatorial notes are written in first-person plural ("We selected this artifact because...")
- Exhibit intros establish historical context before presenting artifacts
- No promotional language anywhere on the site
- Captions explain significance, not just description
- The about page reads as an institutional statement, not a personal pitch

---

## 3. Authority — Cialdini Persuasion Principle

### What Authority does
People trust and follow credible experts. When a source demonstrates expertise and legitimacy, visitors are more likely to engage deeply, spend more time, and treat the content as reliable.

### Applied to this site

**Source attribution**
- Every exhibit cites at least two authoritative sources (Computer History Museum, Britannica, Smithsonian)
- Citations appear inline and in a references section at the bottom of each exhibit

**Curatorial language**
- "This collection was assembled from..." signals curation, not aggregation
- Artifact labels include provenance information where available
- Era timelines reference documented historical events, not opinions

**Visual credibility**
- Clean, structured layouts signal institutional care
- Consistent typography and spacing communicate professionalism
- No ads, no pop-ups, no engagement bait

**Educational framing**
- Learning objectives appear at the top of each exhibit ("After exploring this exhibit, visitors will understand...")
- This frames the site as a teaching resource, not entertainment

---

## How The Three Work Together

| Layer | Framework | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Visual | Bauhaus | Structural clarity, timeless aesthetic, functional beauty |
| Psychological | The Sage | Trust, depth, educational authority |
| Behavioral | Authority | Engagement, time-on-page, return visits |

A visitor arrives and sees a clean, structured layout (Bauhaus). They read calm, precise curatorial language (Sage). They notice source citations and institutional framing (Authority). Each layer reinforces the others. The result is a site that feels like a real museum exhibit — not a student project.
